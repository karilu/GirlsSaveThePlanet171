Food and Climate Change

Food and climate are two things we generally experience only passively. The goal of this project is for users to engage more meaningfully with trends of food production and the energy portfolios of various countries in hopes that they realize that as things are now, we are headed towards an increasingly unequal world.

In the following section we will present a short survey, the results of which will be used to generate starting filters for our first figure. There is no previous knowledge required and you are free to skip it if short on time.

An Uncertain Future

As shown in figure 1, there are many ways our future can change and not every country will be affected in the same way. Naturally, not every country's actions are contributing equally to these hypothetical futures either. The previous projections were created by estimating future atmospheric carbon levels, frequency of precipitation, and average temperature. A deciding factor in which of these scenarios comes to be is the quantity of fossil fuel emissions, which you will be able to explore in the next slide.

Going Green

As shown in figure 1 and 2, global warming impacts worldwide crop production, and fossil fuel emission is one of the most significant culprit. Since the 2000s, the world has ramped up investments in clean energy (biomass, geothermal, hydropower, solar, and wind). For specific investment trend in different regions, please scroll down to see the visualization below.

Figure 3. Stacked Area Chart Illustrating Investment in Clean Energy

Since 2005, world investment in clean energy has increased almost 5 times. China became the largest investor in clean energy,investing 83.4 billion USD in 2019. United States and Europe are the second and third biggest investors. However, the global investment slowed down in the past four years (2015-2019), floating at around 300 billion USD each year. This fact is concerning, as growing emissions will not only impact food production, but also raise sea levels and the possibility of extreme weathers, putting tens and thousands of human lives at risk.


Is zero emission possible?

To avoid the worst consequence of climate change, we will need to reach "net zero" emissions by 2050 or sooner. Net Zero means that, on balance, no more carbon is dumped into the atmosphere than is taken out. To achieve that, the world needs to shift its energy portfolio away from fossil fuels to the greener and cleaner energy. YOU Can Help

To slow down climate change and save our planet (and your favorite food), we cannot rely solely on our governments. There are several easy actions each of us could take.
The number one thing we could do is to reduce food waste. Surprisingly, reducing food waste has the most substantive impact on reducing carbon emissions. Meanwhile, eating a plant-rich diet and choosing public transportation or walking to the destination will also keep our planet cooler.
